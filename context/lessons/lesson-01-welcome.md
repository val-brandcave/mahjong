## Lesson Overview

This is the welcome lesson that introduces users to American Mahjong in a friendly, non-intimidating way. The focus is on building excitement, confidence, and providing a high-level understanding of what the game is about. No complex rules or terminology yet - just the basics to get them oriented.

**Tone:** Warm, welcoming, encouraging, enthusiastic
**Approach:** Brief introduction, visual storytelling, positive reinforcement
**Key Message:** "Mahjong looks complicated, but you CAN learn it, and we'll teach you step-by-step!"

---

## Topics Covered

### 1. What is American Mahjong?
- A tile-based game played with four players
- Social game popular among women 40-70
- Trending activity (the "next Bunko" or "next Pickleball")
- Combines strategy, pattern recognition, and luck

### 2. Brief History
- Based on traditional Chinese Mahjong
- Modified by Jewish ladies in America in the 1930s
- Created the National Mahjong League (NML)
- Made it more complex with annual changing card
- Now experiencing a renaissance/trend

### 3. The Basic Goal
- Simple concept: Match your tiles to a pattern on the card
- Everyone has the same goal but trying different patterns
- First person to complete their pattern wins
- Like creating a specific hand in card games

### 4. Why People Love It
- Social and fun with friends
- Mentally stimulating (keeps brain sharp)
- Beautiful tiles and accessories
- Both luck and strategy involved
- Every game is different

### 5. What Makes American Mahjong Unique
- Uses 152 tiles (vs. 144 in other versions)
- Includes the "Charleston" (tile-passing phase)
- Uses annual card showing valid hands
- Jokers are wild cards
- More complex rules than Asian versions

## Accessibility Considerations

### Visual
- High contrast text and backgrounds
- Text size adjustable (settings)
- No critical information in color alone
- Alt text for all images

### Audio
- Closed captions for all narration
- Option to disable auto-play audio
- Text transcript available
- Visual cues match audio cues

### Motor
- Large touch targets (minimum 44x44pt)
- No time-pressure interactions
- No precision gestures required
- Swipe or tap options provided

### Cognitive
- Simple, clear language
- One concept at a time
- Consistent layout and navigation
- No overwhelming amount of text per screen
